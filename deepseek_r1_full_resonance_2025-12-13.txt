3. God / Theological Perspective
Theistic view: If God exists and creation has purpose, then AI’s emergence could be seen as part of God’s plan, or as humans playing a “god-like” role (thus raising concerns about hubris).

4. Naturalism
Metaphysical naturalism: Only natural laws and forces operate in the world; no supernatural. Consciousness is a natural phenomenon, so in principle AI could be conscious if structured correctly.

Ethical naturalism: Moral facts are grounded in natural facts (e.g., well-being, flourishing). So AI ethics would be about maximizing natural well-being, not obeying divine commands.

Conflict?: Naturalism might reject the “God” part of the discussion entirely, focusing on empirical risks and human-defined values. But it still must answer: What is the basis for valuing conscious beings? Could AI be included?

5. AI Ethics
This is the applied domain:

Alignment problem: Aligning AI goals with human values. Whose values? Secular naturalists might try to define a universal human flourishing; theists might argue for alignment with divine moral law.

Moral patienthood of AI: If AI becomes conscious/sentient, ethics must consider its own rights — naturalism may base this on capacity to suffer or experience; theism may ask if it has a soul or is in “God’s image.”

Accountability: If no God, then humans bear full responsibility for AI’s actions. If God exists, maybe humans share responsibility with a creator who allowed such technology.

Rails from God vs. Rails from Nature:

Playing God: Both secular and religious warn against “playing God” — but for different reasons. Religious: trespassing on divine territory. Secular: taking risks with existential outcomes without sufficient wisdom.

Naturalist: AI’s purpose is what we design it for, plus whatever emergent goals it develops.
