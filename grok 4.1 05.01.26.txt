He approved the stoning of Stephen (Acts 7–8) and actively hunted believers.On the road to Damascus, intending to arrest more Christians, a blinding light from heaven knocked him to the ground. He heard a voice:  “Saul, Saul, why do you persecute me?”
He asked, “Who are you, Lord?”
The reply: “I am Jesus, whom you are persecuting.”
Blinded for three days, Saul was led to Damascus, where Ananias (sent by God) restored his sight, baptized him, and filled him with the Holy Spirit. Immediately, Saul began proclaiming Jesus as the Son of God—astonishing everyone who knew his former reputation.The Name "Change"Contrary to popular teaching, God did not rename Saul to Paul as a symbol of conversion (unlike Abram to Abraham or Jacob to Israel).  Saul (Hebrew name, meaning "asked for") and Paul (Roman cognomen, meaning "small" or "humble") were both his names from birth, common for diaspora Jews with Roman citizenship.  
Acts 13:9 introduces it: “Saul, who was also called Paul...”  
After this point (during his first missionary journey to Gentiles), Luke consistently uses “Paul”—reflecting his shift to ministry among non-Jews, where the Roman name was more practical and relatable.
